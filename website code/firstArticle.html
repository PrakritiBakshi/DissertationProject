The Amaravati Marbles: A story of possession and repatriation

By Prakriti Bakshi / 10 October 2022

Within hours of Queen Elizabeth II’s death in September, Indian Twitter was talking about the Kohinoor: one of the largest cut diamonds in the world that was mined in India and which is now part of the Crown Jewels.

The diamond was last in the news in 2016, when India’s Supreme Court ruled that the country had no claim to the diamond since it had not been taken by force. When the British Prime Minister David Cameron was asked about it on his 2010 tour of India, he said “If you say yes to one, you suddenly find the British Museum would be empty.” This is the floodgates argument against restitution which implies that returning one contested object would set precedent for a deluge of other claims.

But in August the first repatriation of objects to India from the UK took place, when seven antiquities were handed over — not by the British Museum, but by Glasgow Life Museums.

India has only ever made one valid official request to the British Museum for the repatriation of cultural treasures: when the state of Andhra Pradesh asked for the Amaravati Marbles, in 2015.

The request appears to be without response.

What are the Amaravati Marbles?

When the East India Company annexed the Guntur District of modern day Andhra Pradesh in the late 1780s, it set into motion a series of events that led to the discovery, dismemberment, and eventual display of a 2nd century Buddhist stupa at the British Museum. The Amaravati Marbles are a collection of about 121 intricately carved sculptures and narrative relief panels that made up a significant portion of the stupa – a dome edifice used for the veneration of saints and relics. This collection only forms part of the structure with remnants of the shrine in over 20 museums across the world. They are also known as the Elliot Marbles owing to their association with Sir Walter Elliot, a Scottish civil servant of the Madras Presidency, who oversaw a significant period of excavation in 1845. The sculptures are figurative and depict the Buddhist Jataka tales.

The stupa at Amaravati, known as the Mahachaitya or Great Shrine, was one of the most revered Buddhist sites of ancient India. While Buddhism entered a gradual state of decline in south-east India around the 4th century, Amaravati held its esteem as late as the 14th century. As it fell into disuse, this monument of ornate stone carvings was obscured from sight for 400 years. The history of its modern discovery is marked by a series of disparate excavations; some by locals for building material, others by colonial surveyors with varying degrees of excavation skills, often in search of treasure to ship off to private or museum collections.

Historian Upinder Singh summarises the shrine’s fate in her article Amaravati: the dismembering of the Mahācaitya. She says, “the result of a century and three-quarters of explorations and excavations at Amaravati is that today, the site of one of the greatest mahacaityas of ancient India is marked by a nondescript mound ringed by a few forlorn stones.”

The shrine was discovered by a local landlord in 1798 and first documented by Colonel Colin Mackenzie, a military engineer and surveyor. It took about 175 years for these priceless pieces to be safely and respectfully exhibited. During this time the pieces have been refashioned as monuments, carted off to private collections with suboptimal conditions, left out on lawns, stuck to the outer walls of museums, left to languish in stables and packed up for years in basements. The Elliot Marbles in the British Museum are not marble, but limestone. Sir Walter Elliot was neither the architect nor discoverer of the ancient Buddhist stupa, but one of the excavators who shipped off sculptures from the site. The name does, however, capture the controversy that comes to mind when thinking of the similar sounding but much renowned Elgin Marbles from ancient Greece. The Amaravati Collection, as is the more precise description, has had a tumultuous journey from the Indian state of Andhra Pradesh to its current resting place in room 33A of the British Museum. The marbles are described by the museum as, “the single most important group of Indian sculptures outside the subcontinent.”

The state government of Andhra Pradesh now wants them back.

Requests and their status

A Freedom of Information request was sent to the British Museum in July to investigate how many requests for repatriation were made by India since the year 2000. Although the debate, demands and diplomatic quips have never died down, only one valid request was filed from India. The Museum responded that two additional requests were received from India for items not in possession of the museum. The first request in 2003 by the Bharatiya Janata Party of Madhya Pradesh misidentified the Ambika statue as the Saraswati statue invalidating the claim. The second request in 2018 was by the High Commission of India for objects associated with Rani Chennamma, but these were not held by the British Museum.

The one credible request for the Amaravati sculptures seems to be a one-man mission rather than strategic national policy. In 2015, Veeranjaneyulu Jasti, the chairman of the Amaravati Development Authority wrote a letter to Prime Minister Narendra Modi urging the Centre to take necessary steps to bring back the Amaravati Collection from London. The same year he’d also written a letter to Tamil Nadu Chief Minister J Jayalalithaa seeking the return of hundreds of Amaravati sculptures housed at the National Museum at Chennai. The artefacts had been shipped to erstwhile Madras by the British during colonial rule. Mr. Jasti seemed to be the person behind this mission. While the Tamil Nadu government agreed to return the objects, the question of the Amaravati Marbles is shrouded in silence. There has been no known response nor follow-up.

The Andhra Pradesh government has ambitious plans for the city of Amaravati which was made the new capital of Andhra Pradesh in 2015. An integral part of this plan was to make the city a tourist hub by bringing back lost cultural heritage by establishing a ‘Monastery Boulevard’ – a place of solace for Buddhists from across the world. It has been 7 years since the request was filed and now the trail has gone cold.

Seven months before this repatriation request was filed, the Indian media and public were lambasting the solicitor general’s comments in the Supreme Court that renounced any claim India had to the Kohinoor. In response, the government declared its intention to make all possible efforts to bring back the gem in an amicable way. The ministers of foreign affairs and culture were consulted to discuss potential strategies and one of the options was to reportedly relinquish all rights to artefacts in possession of the UK for return of the Kohinoor. This would include the Amaravati Marbles.

Even with fresh instances of repatriation, the Kohinoor back in the news, and renewed talk on colonialism, the Amaravati Marbles failed to reach headlines with no new spokesperson for their case.

Prashant Reddy Thikkavarapu summarises the need of the hour in his article A Rock and a Hard Place published in The Caravan; “If India is to recover the Kohinoor or anything else, what the country needs is not a quick compromise of the kind the government considered, but rather a long-term diplomatic strategy that expressly links demands for restitution to the immorality of colonialism.” he says. The article was published 7 years ago and still holds. Even in the recent fervour on the Kohinoor, no strategic long-term plan was proposed from either side of the table to address the injustices of the colonial era.

Precedent for return

The British Museum owns 8 million objects of which only 1% are on permanent display. The collection from pre-independent India comprises 65,336 objects. That, however, does not mean that all of these objects are looted artefacts. Nor do they all have the cultural and religious value that the Amaravati Marbles do. Therefore, those in favour of restitution maintain that objects that have been contested or have had a tumultuous history should be acknowledged without the fear of the floodgates argument.

The UK maintains that India has no legal claim over objects taken into possession during colonial rule. Looking at the country’s position in history, the Allied governments issued the Inter-Allied Declaration Against Acts of Dispossession Committed in Territories Under Enemy Occupation or Control in 1943. This stated their intent to invalidate any transfer of property regardless of their “legality”. The default stance was that transfer of property in occupied territory was under duress. The declaration holds “whether transfers or dealings have taken the form of open looting or plunder, or of transactions apparently legal in form, even when they purport to be voluntarily effected." The principle on which this declaration stands is that property taken under coercive circumstances should be returned to their country of origin. The baseline is whether or not the UK admits the illegality or colonial rule.

It is not that the UK does not believe in restitution. The Holocaust (Return of Cultural Objects) Act 2009 that allows the deaccession by museums and the return of cultural objects acquired during the Nazi era to their rightful owners was renwed indefinitely in 2019. The British Museum also has a carefully drafted repatriation policy for human remains. However, there is no law for the repatriation of loot that had come to the UK from its former colonies.

The repatriation discourse was reignited in 2020 when President Emmanual Macron promised to return all African artefacts acquired from colonies of the French empire. The trend has led to the first restitution made by the UK to India. Six of the seven items returned by Glasgow Museums were forcibly taken from temples in North India and the seventh, a sword, was looted in 1905 from the collection of the ruler of Hyderabad. The items were gifted to the Scotland museums and have now found their way home a century later. The Amaravati Marbles await a similar fate.
