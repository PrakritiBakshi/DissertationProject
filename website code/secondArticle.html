The Language of Skin Lightening Creams in India

By Prakriti Bakshi / 13 October 2022

The multi-billion dollar fairness cream industry in India sells fairness as a prerequisite for beauty, confidence, and success. While there is the direct correlation of British companies like Hindustan Unilever profiting off of an industry that exemplifies colourism, the need for such an industry has colonial antecedents itself. Although a colour-based power imbalance existed before colonial rule, there is little evidence to show that discrimination was metted out solely due to skin colour. The underlying reason was caste—a hierarchical system in society that demarcated clear division of labour. With lower caste persons working in the sun for long durations and having lesser economic and social capital, society remained further stratified on the basis of colour, caste, and class. India’s reverence for lighter skin was exacerbated with the arrival of imperial rule. As the British strengthened their position, power’s association with lighter skin strengthened.

The murder of George Floyd on 25 May 2020 and the subsequent Black Lives Matter (BLM) protests around the globe renewed the discourse on racism. Many Bollywood celebrities were called out for their hypocrisy of speaking out against racism in a time of global protest while promoting skin lightening products back home. The resulting backlash led to companies rushing to rebrand their fairness products in June 2020. The biggest names in the market began company wide rebranding without addressing the prevalence of colourism. Overnight, ‘fair’ became ‘glow’ and ‘white’ became ‘bright’. The rebranding seems to be merely skin-deep.

Two years on, have the companies really stopped using the words ‘fair’ and ‘white’? Is it enough? With the products still on shelves and the industry still profitable, has anything really changed apart from the codification of certain words to mean fair?

An analysis in 4 charts

We looked at 500 skin cream search results on Amazon to see if they are still using the words ‘fair’ and/or ‘white’. The results were derived from 4 keywords that are alternatives for fairness creams: Lightening cream, Brightening cream, Illuminating cream, and Glow cream.

The results reveal that despite promises by many skin cream brands to shift to stop using the words ‘fair’ and ‘white’ 30% of the skin products still used them to describe their products. Despite promises of diversity, nearly 60% of skin products had light skin models in their advertisements. Reviews and questions on these Amazon results illustrate the gravity of the situation and just how deep-seated colourism is. On a product listing of a fairness cream that had stopped calling itself a fairness cream, an anonymous user had asked on 15th July 2022 if the product was good for her 13 year old daughter who had dark skin. A user of one of the radiance creams complained that they were still the same shade despite using the product regularly for 2 months. The anecdotes go on.

Matrimonial advertisements boasting and requesting fairness reflect the way people think in our society. The influence is not from the platform hosting the classified advertisements but from the people. With fairness cream advertisements it's a perpetual cycle that needs disruption.

This is a problem that needs more from the industry than thesaurus fuelled rebranding.
